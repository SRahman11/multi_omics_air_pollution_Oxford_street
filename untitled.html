Introduction

The causal association between long-term negative health outcomes and air pollution is well established in the literature \cite{Cohen_2005,Beelen_2014,Burnett_2014,Tuomisto_2008,Pope_III_2002}. The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that 4.2 million people die each year as a direct consequence of ambient (outdoor) air pollution \cite{pollution}, and traffic-related air pollutants (TRAPs) make up a large proportion of this pollution.
Short-term exposure to TRAPs has also been shown to have acute negative health effects, especially in patients with pre-existing respiratory or cardiac conditions \cite{McCreanor_2007,Laumbach_2014,Cole_Hunter_2015}. The advent in the 21st century of high-throughput OMICs technologies has facilitated the investigation of associations between environmental exposures and biological responses at a cellular level, with the result that biological pathways can be mapped with great precision, and the links between exposure and outcome examined in detail.
In this study, we investigate the effects of short-term exposure to air pollution at a multi-OMIC level. Measured exposures are five TRAP particles: PM10, PM25, black carbon (CBLK), nitrogen dioxide (NO2) and ultrafine particles (PCNT). OMICs responses are analysed at the transcriptomic level (mRNA and miRNA), the metabolomic level and the adductomic level,  from which the results are subsequently applied in a multi-OMICs integrative analysis to uncover any biological significance. 

Methods

This study used a randomised crossover interventional study design. A cohort of 119 patients were recruited to the trial; 40 of these patients were healthy and were recruited by advertising in the Royal Brompton Hospital; 40 had chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) and 39 had ischaemic heart disease (IHD). The patients with pre-existing health conditions were recruited from existing databases or outpatient clinics at the Royal Brompton & Harefield NHS Foundation Trust.\cite{Sinharay_2018}.
Details on sex, age
Each participant in the study was randomly assigned to one of two locations – Oxford Street or Hyde Park – in London, and driven to that location by electric car. They then walked for two hours in their allotted location, pausing to rest for 15 minutes every half an hour. The total distance walked was approximately 6 kilometres. Between 3 and 8 weeks later, the participants were taken to the other location and completed the same distance and duration of walk.

Exposure measurement

At the start and end of each walk session, concentrations of five TRAP particles were gauged using appropriate particulate matter measurement devices (full details can be found in \cite{Sinharay_2018}).
Average baseline levels of exposure to the five TRAP particles were generated for each participant using a modelling process,  and the differences between the modelled baseline levels and the measured exposure levels, accounting for a lagging effect, were used in the analysis.
