Abstract

Introduction

While the association between long-term negative health outcomes and air pollution has been thoroughly investigated, short term exposure to air pollution and traffic-related air pollutants (TRAPs) remains largely unexplored. In this study we investigate the effects of short- term exposure to air pollution at a multi-omic level.

Methods

Using a randomised crossover interventional study design, recruited participants were allocated to walk for two hours at one of two sites –Hyde Park or Oxford Street in Central London – where measurements of exposures and and blood serum samples were collected before and after the walk. Univariate models were used to investigate the association between 5 reported TRAPS (NO2, CO2, PM2.5, PM10 and CBLK) and biomarkers at four OMICs levels. Network analysis and partial least squares regression were used to investigate and visualise the relationships between significant markers at different OMIC levels.

Introduction

The causal association between long-term negative health outcomes and air pollution is well established in the literature\cite{Cohen_2005,Beelen_2014,Burnett_2014,Tuomisto_2008,Pope_III_2002}. In 2015, air pollution and  outdoor particulate matter (PM) was designated as a human carcinogen by the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC)\cite{Hamra2014}. Long term exposure to air pollution and TRAPs has been associated with a variety of human conditions, ranging from asthma to cancer of the lungs \cite{Guarnieri2014}. The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that 4.2 million people die each year as a direct consequence of ambient (outdoor) air pollution\cite{pollution}, and traffic-related air pollutants (TRAPs) make up a large proportion of this pollution.
Short-term exposure to TRAPs has also been shown to have acute negative health effects, especially in patients with pre-existing respiratory or cardiac conditions \cite{McCreanor_2007,Laumbach_2014,Cole_Hunter_2015}. The advent in the 21st century of high-throughput OMICs technologies has facilitated the investigation of associations between environmental exposures and biological responses at a cellular level, with the result that biological pathways can be mapped with precision, and the links between exposure and outcome examined in detail. Recent research has therefore begun to focus on investigating the effects of TRAP exposure at a molecular level, using OMICs-derived methods of computational and statistical analysis \cite{van_Veldhoven_2019,Alfano_2018,Mostafavi_2018,Esp_n_P_rez_2018,Krauskopf_2018,Krauskopf_2018a}.
In this study, we investigate the effects of short-term exposure to air pollution at a multi-OMIC level. Measured exposures are five TRAP particles: PM10, PM25, black carbon (CBLK), nitrogen dioxide (NO2) and ultrafine particles (PCNT). Molecular responses are analysed at the transcriptomic level (mRNA and miRNA), the metabolomic level and the adductomic level,  and a multi-OMICs integrative analysis is conducted to attempt to identify evidence of biological mechanisms linking OMICs levels.

Methods

This study used a randomised crossover interventional study design. A cohort of 119 patients were recruited to the trial: 40 of these patients were healthy and were recruited by advertising in the Royal Brompton Hospital; 40 had chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) and 39 had ischaemic heart disease (IHD). The patients with pre-existing health conditions were recruited from existing databases or outpatient clinics at the Royal Brompton & Harefield NHS Foundation Trust.\cite{Sinharay_2018}.
Each participant in the study was randomly assigned to one of two locations – Oxford Street or Hyde Park – in London, and driven to that location by electric car. They then walked for two hours in their allotted location, pausing to rest for 15 minutes every half an hour. The total distance walked was approximately 6 kilometres. Between 3 and 8 weeks later, the participants were taken to the other location and completed the same distance and duration of walk.

Exposure measurement

At the start and end of each walk session, atmospheric concentrations of five TRAP particles were gauged using appropriate particulate matter measurement devices (full details can be found in Sinhray et al, 2018\cite{Sinharay_2018}).
